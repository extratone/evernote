How To – Become A Motoring Journalist

Written by:Carlton Boyce
Written on:May 11, 2012
Comments are closed

So, you want to be a motoring journalist, do you? Drawn, no doubt, by the lure of a fast buck, lavish foreign launch events, and access to more new cars than you could possibly drive in one lifetime.

Ignoring the fact that the life of a motoring journo just isn’t like that, where on earth do you start?

Well, you could go back to university as both Cardiff and Coventry offer well-respected courses in motoring journalism – but even they would accept that there are many paths into the profession, few of which require qualifications. If you want to study to gain new skills then you are wise beyond your years, but don’t expect either course to offer any short cuts, just a solid grounding in the basics of the job…

If there is a short cut (and I remain unconvinced) then it is the Sir William Lyons Award, run every year by the Guild of Motoring Writers. Open to aspiring writers aged 17-23, it offers a first prize of £2,000 and lots of publicity. Previous winners have generally done very well but competition is fierce and it is aimed at the younger journalist rather than someone who is changing careers in their middle years as I did.

Practical training and advice can be found through headlineauto’s annual Masterclass, which offers potential motoring journos the chance to drive a range of new cars on the famous Top Gear track at Dunsfold. Previous attendees speak very positively about it and, in addition to keynote speeches by industry luminaries, it offers something that few others do: practical advice on how to evaluate a car’s dynamic behaviour.

But don’t be misled; how to evaluate a car accurately might be a key skill, but it probably isn’t the most important one. Every successful motoring journalist is a skilful writer who just happens to write about cars, and no-one explains this better than the former editor of CAR magazine, Mel Nichols. Mel, and CAR, have an enviable reputation for the quality of their writing; it wouldn’t be an exaggeration to say that CAR magazine was the most influential publication of its type for thirty years and its success was entirely down to the quality of its contributors. All were obsessed with the art of writing – and not all were obsessed with cars.

The most common route into motoring journalism these days is via your own blog. Start by writing about your car, no matter how old, or how humble. Talk about the industry, new cars, government initiatives, trends and developments. Join Twitter and follow those journalists you respect and admire. Link to your work and, if it’s any good, they might retweet it. But remember, social media is a long-term relationship, not date rape. You must engage, build friendships, offer help and advice, and be patient.

Write often, and find your own voice. Too many writers (me included) write in the same formulaic way. If you can, get hold of a few copies of CAR magazine from the late 1970s and 1980s and read Russell Bulgin, Phil Llewellin, and George Bishop. All of them could tell a story that had nothing whatsoever to do with the way a car is engineered or how it drives. By contrast, LJK Setright was a master at describing why a car’s engineering was flawed and, despite being impenetrable at times, he was a compelling writer who generated light as well as heat.

Other notable writers include Alan Clark and P.J. O’Rourke, neither of whom are motoring journalists in the purest sense but both of whom write beautifully about cars.

Don’t ignore Jeremy Clarkson, either. He might have degenerated into a caricature of himself but he is, at heart, a fine motoring journalist who writes prodigiously and fluently. In fact, most of the motoring journalists who write for the weekend papers are brilliant at writing interesting articles about cars that are, sometimes, no better than mundane.

All of these writers found their own voice, their own way of approaching a car, their own priorities – and all of them were able to write on any subject and make it interesting. It isn’t easy, but try to find the third way. If you do you’ll never be short of work.

You’ll probably have to work for free initially too (which is good experience for when you do start to get paid, because you’ll only ever earn a pittance). Contact car magazines, websites, and blogs offering yourself as an unpaid intern for a week or so. Link to your blog, so they can see the quality of your writing. If you are successful you’ll get contacts out of it, bylines, and experience.

I learned more writing news articles for a well-known motoring outlet than anything else I have done. If nothing else, you’ll realise that writing about the mild facelift of a nondescript car is a soul-destroying experience, but one that you’ll have to do several times a week. This work experience will help you get a paid gig, after which you’ll be moving through the industry like a shark. Possibly.

Don’t forget about the photographs, either. (After a while, when you’re rich, and famous, and writing for a Sunday broadsheet, they’ll send you a photographer. The rest of us have to make do with taking our own pictures.) A common mistake is to focus on the writing, ignoring the fact that every article, feature, and news story needs a good photograph (or six) to support it. You don’t need an expensive camera, but you do need to understand some key concepts. It isn’t rocket science, but it takes time to get used to, so practice, practice, and then practice some more. Oh, and buy James Mann’s book How To Photograph Cars. It isn’t cheap, but it is good.

Getting hold of cars to test is easy once you’re established, but it’s almost impossible when you’re just starting out. Car manufacturers have a vested interest in letting motoring journalists drive their cars and a finely-honed nose for bullshit, so honesty is your best bet. They’ll generally be extremely helpful and positive if you admit that you are new to the job, guiding you towards alternative ways to get your bum onto one of their cars. After that, it’s up to you.

To make the grade as a full-time motoring journalist you’ll need to be persistent and develop the skin of a rhino. It will probably take you years to get established and you’ll be knocked down more times than Kerry Katona by people who tell you that it can’t be done.

But, it can and when you’re out driving in the sun, or talking nonsense with your colleagues, or writing in the small hours, there is no better job in the world.

It just doesn’t pay very much. OK?
