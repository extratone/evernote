Roxy's wristband, nationwide events spark downtown protest

Management at Roxy’s bar and dance club apologized on Facebook late Friday for a “hands up, pants up” wristband that sparked a late night protest linked to police involvement in the deaths of black men in Ferguson and New York.

A group of around 90 protesters linked arms and stopped traffic outside of Roxy’s at 1025 E. Broadway at 11:30 p.m. Friday, eventually causing police to close the intersection of Tenth and Cherry streets to vehicle traffic for a short time.

Many protesters said the bar’s wristband, which Roxy’s managers said was meant to make light of the club’s own dress code policy, sounded too much like a play on the “hands up, don’t shoot” slogan that has accompanied numerous protests -- some violent -- across the country to protest the Aug. 9 killing of Ferguson Michael Brown. A grand jury decided not to indict Darren Wilson, the white police officer who shot Brown, prompting widespread protests, violence and looting on Nov. 24.

The Facebook message for Roxy’s, posted late Friday, said the wristband phrase “and several similar varieties” have been used for more than a year. The message said the wristband used at the door Friday night “was clearly in poor taste.”

“After seeing the wristbands, something was ignited in me,” demonstration organizer and University of Missouri student Ayanna Poole said in a Facebook message. Poole lost her voice during the demonstration. “It tore my community apart that morning reading those wristbands, and I couldn’t stand on the sidelines anymore.”

The Tribune was unable to reach anyone at Roxy’s for comment.

The Facebook apology said the wristbands were meant to be a joke about the bar’s dress code policy, which forbids “extremely saggy pants” and forbids entry to anyone whose pants won’t stay up without the use of their hands. But many of the protesters felt this apology was not enough.

“Even regardless of those events” in Ferguson “the notion is still deeply problematic, informal policing of people’s bodies,” Christopher Williams, who attended the protest, said. “For me something like sagging pants, I fall into that group. ... You’re talking about me. You’re talking about people who are my family, too. Their apology was in light of the events that happened in Ferguson. It wasn’t ‘what we said was wrong, and we shouldn’t be trying to police people’s bodies.’ ”

Roxy’s Facebook apology said the wristbands “in no way are meant to make light of current events.”

At one point during Friday night’s protest, the protesters laid down in the street for four and a half minutes, with each minute representing each hour that Brown’s body was left on a street in Ferguson after being killed.

“People stepped over me,” Williams said. “I was laying in the streets, Broadway and Hitt Street, and one passerby tried to flick a cigarette on me.”

Poole said one drunk woman tried to run straight through the line of protesters when their arms were locked. Williams said he heard several people shout the n-word at the group.

“I think right now Columbia is in a very fragile state,” he said.

Protesters also put their hands in the air, evoking the “hands up, don’t shoot” chants that have become commonplace in demonstrations across the country.

“People were kind of lowering themselves onto their knees and putting their hands behind their heads kind of symbolizing that even in that vulnerable position, there’s a high risk,” said Payton Huse, an MU junior who was at the protest.

She said she saw mixed reactions from the downtown crowd of observers. Huse said she heard some people make racist comments, and watched some people step over protesters who were laying down. But she also saw people come out of bars to join the protesters.

Poole said the group of people she was with kept her going, even when she felt discouraged.

“We built a family out there,” she said. “Everytime some would try to physically attack the crowd, we’d stop it and go to the middle of the street to pray. At times I was overwhelmed, when I noticed people thought it was a joking matter I’d break out in tears, but the people fighting with me reassured me that it was all worth it.”
