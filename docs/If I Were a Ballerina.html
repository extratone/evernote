If I Were a Ballerina

By Kelly Conaboy

  March 17, 2016
Photograph by Dave J. Hogan / Getty

Deep down, I’ve always known that I have what it takes to be a ballerina. In fact, when I see other ballerinas—onstage, or in paintings, or if there’s someone who looks like she could be a ballerina on the train, or in a painting—it’s hard not to feel a little smug. Here these guys are, thinking they’re so great; and here I am, confident that I would be better.

For example, here’s something I’d do if I were a ballerina: I’d know everything there is to know about ballet. You’d quiz me on a ballet fact and I’d know it instantly. Something like, “Who was the first person to blah, blah, ballet something?” Before you even finished asking your question, I’d say, “This person.” And you’d look up the answer and I would be right! Every time.

If I were a ballerina, I wouldn’t broadcast it. Equal parts modest and lithe, I would grand jeté to and fro, here and there. I would grand jeté all around town. You’re probably thinking, “But wouldn’t that let people know that you were a ballerina right away?” Jesus. Listen, I would grand jeté very quietly, all right? A pin could drop and, before you heard my grand jeté, you’d hear the pin. So quietly would I grand jeté that if you noticed and thought something of it—like, maybe that I was a ballerina, and you happened to be impressed by that—well, that would be on you, then, wouldn’t it? For looking?

Similarly, I wouldn’t bring up being a ballerina out of nowhere in conversation. I’d wait to be prompted. Like, at work, Jessica would ask—the way she does, without fail, every single Monday—“What did you get up to over the weekend?” Oh, here we go. I would say, “Ballet,” and she would say, “I didn’t know you did ballet.” (And in her head, she’s thinking, Ballet? On top of everything else?!) I would say, “Oh, yeah, I’ve done ballet for a while—I’m actually a ballerina.” Then I’d “forget” to ask her what she did over the weekend and walk away doing a ballet move, because of muscle memory.

And here’s something else. If I were a ballerina, I would take fate into my own hands. Rather than wait for someone else to suggest a ballet contest at a party, I would go to parties and whisper “ballet contest” into the ears of all the guests. Then, because of the power of suggestion, someone else would pick up on the idea, and think it was their own. “I just had a great idea,” the most easily manipulated of the party guests would say. “Let’s have a ballet contest.” Ha! Oh my god. This guy is playing right into my hands.

(I would win.)

If I were a ballerina, graceful and refined, I’d work tirelessly at my craft. Day and night I would practice in an unadorned dance studio. “Why do you work so hard, when you’re already an incredible ballerina?” friends would ask. I’d smile and respond, with unmistakable ballerina-like poise, “My friend, how do you think I became an incredible ballerina? By lying around, like some lazy piece of shit? Sitting on my ass like some garbage person?” Jesus. “Pieces of my body just rotting and falling off, because all I ever did was sit there like some slug? Is that how you think I became an incredible ballerina?” And then I’d cup my hand to my ear and lean in real close like I was straining to hear my friend’s response. I’d get closer and closer to her face until my cupped hand was right on her mouth, waiting.

I would also do charity ballet things, if I were a ballerina. (Put a tutu on a shelter dog; teach a tutu-wearing shelter dog to plié; enter a tutu-wearing plié shelter dog in a contest; win.) I’d ask for no recognition or even payment, because the opportunity to help others would be its own reward, etc. And then Jessica from work would hear about all the good I was doing on top of being an incredible ballerina and she’d be like, “Arrgh!”

Yes, if I were a ballerina, even my very worst enemies would have to admit: Damn, she’s good at what she does.

(Ballet.)
