Welcome to year's end Extratone, 2016.

2016 - unquestionably the worst time ever to start a website. 2017, though, shall be known as The Year of Extratone, and not just by my mom.

I've done a lot of looking back in these letters, but I guess that's the idea, right? I'm never sure how much I'd like to repeat myself. I'm not sure how much more reflection really needs doing - at least for the moment.

The short of it:
  • May: The website is born, Alek Michael posts our first and only listicle
  • July: 2.0 is launched, the Net's format as it exists today begins to take shape, The New (now Futureland) begins.
  • August: brief bit of absurdity from contributors, podcasts continue to expand
  • September: 3.0 is launched, Isiah graces us with four entertaining features.
  • October: an audio renaissance - Web Crates begins, The Movie Closet arrives.
  • November: The New reimagined into Futureland, Production Editor Emily Waters and Chief Technical Officer Cil Ramsey are brought on board.
Jez...

It's seemed so much longer than that. What a hell of a year. Probably the most dense I've yet to live. I am confronted with the image of having to explain to my grandchildren that it took a WordPress site - of all things - to teach me about milestones.

When I launched Extratone, I considered the World Wide Web a mere stepping stone to print. I'd always assumed there was little hope for beauty in experiences on the internet, but my exponential increase in online news consumption due to this project has exposed me to a different standard of digital storytelling. I thought I'd have to create the content I wanted to read in order to see it realized, but my newfound diligence has led me to discover initiatives like The Outline - an extraordinary site with a very complimentary editorial heading.

Courage like theirs is lifegiving and it's clearly present, somewhere. And it's not just the compulsive creation of a 20-something college dropout - it's Joshua Topolsky's vision - a name that is no small deal in the industry. If I recall correctly, I saw him described as a "digital media veteran."

I've certainly got enough to say about The Outline for a URL of its own - it's imminent - but for now, just know that every word I've heard (or read) Topolsky say of it has been keenly identical to what I would say of Extratone if I had his seniority and experience, which makes him and his creation "role models" of sorts. That's new and interesting, but I promise I won't let us become defined by Digital Daddy Issues.
MAGFest 2017
Our upcoming coverage of The Music and Gaming Festival is by far the most prevalent topic for this entry.

Yeah - our little website is traveling across the continent to spend three days absorbing and capturing as much as we possibly can of what's been (very loosely) described as "the new ComicCon." (As you may know, my beef with Wil Wheaton prevents me from ever going to ComicCon, but its loss of youth since its birth has made it irrelevant, anyway.) The announcement should be your first read.

Basically, at least four of our staff will be on the ground with cameras and notebooks (some digital, a few tactile) and a bunch more of our friends will be present, attending and presenting. It's bound to be a Huge Deal no matter what happens.

This sort of thing isn't cheap, though, and we require your financial support. As you've probably noticed, our need has necessitated drastic action (in context, anyway.) If our popup has been disconcerting you, I apologize. It's called Philogus, and it will be killed before we leave on the fifth, assuming I don't lose my temper with it in the meantime. Because I am uninterested in mastering the practice any more than was necessary to birth the thing, it is an indiscriminate menace. It'll even follow you as far as our Privacy Policy, which is probably illegal or something.

Considering this is our first time in one room together, it's most definitely an experiment. If you trust me enough to have read this far, though, know that I expect it to be worth your while. The most effective way to get your donation to us for this particular endeavor is through my PayPal account, but if you're interested in Extratone's long game, you can also patronize (haha) our new Patreon. As I understand it, I think it's a great preface (and/or alternative) to an in-house subscription service. I uh... don't really understand much, though, so help with the profile itself would be a welcome substitution if you're unable to contribute financially.

That said, THANK YOU BEN STOKES.
As you know, a direct shoutout in my letters costs $100, and the genius behind The Movie Closet has just fitted the bill.

Seriously though - The Kind Stokes broke all of our records with his $100 donation, but more importantly... we must do this thing right, now. Don't you dare let him down.

"MAGFest Edition" is now on Drakesville, our test mirror, which now has its own sign-up page, if you'd like to help us keep our child healthy and tidy or need to release the building wrath you've developed against us in a safe environment.

So far, the people you need to follow on Twitter for the Extra scoop are Emily Waters, Wabi, and myself. Nina may take over our Snapchat account, but I'm no so sure publication snap coverage is as common as it was for a moment. We will be podcasting live on our YouTube channel, and shall do our best to give the main roll some immediate attention. The unique stuff, though, will come in the weeks after we've returned and reflected. The photography and written pieces should be somethin' else.
Perhaps you've noticed that Prose Editor-at-Large Tim Marx hasn't been very at-large lately. He came to the rescue when the young people were unavailable to record Futureland, and we ended up signing him up for his Twitter account on the show, which was entertaining. We began recording our Hunter S. Thompson episode of Off The Grid a few days ago, but ended up dancing around the topic because of the much-anticipated appearance of Alexa (Anne Thracks,) who has contributed two superb works of prose. You're gonna wanna keep your eye on her - she's got that voice pretty well mastered.

We also mentioned Tim's unusual plan for 2017, and it now has a (placeholder) title - The Endeavor. Columbia, Missouri - where Extratone headquarters is cordoned - is an achingly-moderate college town full of adderall-hauling students and dull, middle-aged fucks who think themselves awfully quirky. Beneathe all moderate places, though, is a cultural counterweight of some kind, and ours is an unwholesome underground of sexual deviancy.

Before I go on, let me be clear: we are not here to expose anything. We are not investigative journalists or zealous truthists - our only desire from this undertaking is for good stories.

Using friends' networks and Craigslist, we intend to spend two thousand, seventeen actively delving into local fetish communities as much as possible. Though I say "we," I must note that this is Tim's vision & vessel, through and through. It is his to steer where he wants, though I will be tagging along as much as I can. Along the way, he will be posting stories on The Endeavor's roll, which will be compiled afterwards into a book.

Ultimately, neither of us can anticipate what's coming, and we should all be grateful. Scribam quid non legerim - it's gonna be a helluva ride.
Our new Production Editor has been putting in more than her Fair Share. Emily wrote the first of her Poems and Peroxide series. My hope is that her straightforward method will provide substantial relief from my mealymouthed ramblings - both written and spoken. Her participation in Futureland has been helpful, though she did spill french fries on the floor of Studio Eat.

The Movie Closet finished their four-part "favorites" series and recorded a fascinating & concise discussion with the crew behind Under The Glass - a local documentary about the history of pinball. Their Christmas Special is vastly superior to the other holiday-edition podcasts I've heard, as well.


Regardless of the results of just about any possible happening in the next month (except for my own death, perhaps,) the next Freq Check you receive will be vastly different from this and all prior correspondence. I cannot help but continue to thank you for making all of this possible. I am obsessed with making sure our continued transactions are mutually beneficial, and elated by the possibilities in our imminent future.

Happy winter to you.
