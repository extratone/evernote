Possible Topics/Separate Thoughts
  • Bipolar Disorder/Eggshell Syndrome
  • Specific details about my childhood
  • Emerging/recovering from the situation

- Main purpose is to communicate a positive message to the audience.  Find main lessons I've learned, state them sort of like thesis statements, then relate specific stories as evidence. 

-Talk about intent?  Dad had good intentions. 

-Stay on topic.  The essay is not about Dad or I, it's about communicating an inspirational message to the reader.

-Maybe focus on the specific event of Dad telling me he wouldn't be picking me up for awhile as "punishment." 

-Communicate the uncertainty and stress.  The inconsistency.  The misconceptions I'm sure I have about men and people in general because of what I "endured" through childhood. 

-Probably made me afraid of change because I learned to find security in a lack of change.  I couldn't predict change.

-Dad was always changing so I needed a complete lack of change in all the other areas of my life as compensation.

-Dreaded summer because I would spend so much of it at the farm.

-When first seeing Dad after a period of not seeing him, I would constantly search for signs of his state/mood.  Every word and tone was scrutinized.  Body language, facial expression, etc.

-I apologized constantly, as well as thanked constantly, even when not around Dad.

-Main focus (as far as the problems of my childhood,) could be inconsistency and fear of change, although I'm not sure what positive message could be taken from that.  It is the fundamental issue with having a bipolar parent though. (at least in my view.)

-I distinctly remember a hard-to-describe feeling/atmosphere that Dad, and in some cases the family created.  The closest I can come to describing it is a 50s rural farm mentality.  I always felt that I was essentially useless.  It seemed to me that other farm kids were more resilient and always knew what needed to be done.  I think, possibly because of the ADD (which dad would not let me be examined for.  Possibly because he was uncomfortable with acknowledging his own mental problems) I was always spacing out when working with Dad, so i wouldn't know what to do, which would frustrate him.

-Although it may not be particularly relevant, thinking back now, I realize that the duality of my life left me feeling like an outcast on both sides.  On the farm and around farmers, I felt like a "city kid," and I remember (perhaps incorrectly, perhaps not,) Dad speaking about this a few times, maybe even using that terminology.  At school, I felt like I didn't understand the other kids.  Which is somewhat true.  Many things that most kids experienced throughout their childhood, I was not aware of or exposed to.  Video games, T.V. shows, movies, food, etc.  

-I could consider this a good thing though because there will come a point where not experiencing such things will be irrelevant, and I spent the time that would've taken on doing things I would consider beneficial and productive.  (Mechanical stuff, reading, working, selling sweetcorn, etc..)

  • The Mall
-I remember walking fairly far behind dad toward the jewelry store, away from the food court.  I was nervous, as I always was when we were in public with his bad mood, because others did not understand, which would sometimes lead to quarrels. 

  • Dropping me off
-After the mall, Dad drove to mom's house without explaining why (which was disconcerting and unusual.)
-When we arrived in front of the house, dad told me that he would not be picking me up for awhile.  
-I acted disappointed, and I believe that it was fairly convincing.
-I paused deliberately before getting out of the Explorer, making every effort to act as if I didn't want to leave, yet inside, I was so excited that it seemed I'd go blind.

AFTER THE FINAL DRAFT

-Although I originally intended to "show" more than "tell" in this essay, I found the task to be more difficult than it should have been.
-I continually shift perspectives, which could possibly make the story confusing to the reader.  Unfortunately, it is my natural thought process to do so.
-Change all "4s" to "Four"
-Because my mentality and thought processes change constantly, I believe it would be better to write a first draft in one sitting next time, without an outline, and base my final draft on the basic structure of the first draft, but with more detail and vivid language added.
