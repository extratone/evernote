First published on Sunday 17 November 2013 05.00Â EST

From home-baking recipes to luxury car reviews, from diet advice to celebrity interviews, British magazines continue to be the informed friends the public turns to in a quiet moment. This month sees a retrospective look at a century of titles: the good, the bad and the stylish.

If the bleakest predictions had been proved right, the 100th anniversary of the industry's trade body, the Professional Publishers Association, would have been a mournful, nostalgic look back at a bygone business model. Instead, its celebration of key moments from magazine history shows how new trends have taken off while reliable old publications have frequently found a fresh audience.

Advertisement

A brochure, put together by Gill Hudson of Reader's Digest â€“ a former editor of the Radio Times, Company and Maxim â€“ for the association's celebratory event on 21 November, kicks off its survey of 100 pivotal magazine moments with Margaret Thatcher's resonant interview from 1987, when the prime minister told Woman's Own: "There is no such thing as society. There is a living tapestry of men and women and people, and the beauty of that tapestry and the quality of our lives will depend upon how much each of us is prepared to take responsibility for ourselves." The scoop demonstrated that the more relaxed atmosphere of a feature interview can sometimes make more waves than the front pages of a newspaper.

As Thatcher rang the bell for the start of the age of the individual, so Gordon Roddick, the co-founder of the Body Shop, and John Bird were preparing to help those individuals not likely to make it on their own with the launch of the Big Issue in 1991. Produced in response to the growing number of rough sleepers on the streets of the capital, Bird and Roddick's magazine offered an induction course and a fixed pitch on the pavement for an army of homeless sellers. It now works with around 2,000 vulnerable people.

Women's magazines have undergone a design and content revolution in the last century, from the proliferation of catwalk coverage to the huge demand for celebrity-based photo-journalism. While the influential arrival in 1972 of both the feminist title Spare Rib and the straight-talking Cosmopolitan helped give women a stronger voice in society, it is pure fashion and gossip that now drive trade on an unimagined scale.

An attempt to supply the everyday woman with a portion of advice and entertainment that would last no longer than a cup of tea came in 1990 with the launch of Take a Break. Still the bestselling women's magazine in the country, it created a new publishing genre: true-life weeklies. John Dale, who edited the title for 20 years, said: "I held certain beliefs â€“ first, that working-class women, especially in the north, were the victims of institutional discrimination; second, that such women were ill-served by magazines produced by home counties types; third, that these women needed stories about themselves rather than the Queen Mum and Joan Collins; and fourth, that the journalist should get out of the way so women could speak directly to other women in their own voice."

One of the best judged pieces of social class commentary since Nancy Mitford wrote about "U" and "non-U" manners for Encounter magazine in 1955 was hidden between the covers of the October 1975 edition of Harpers & Queen. This is where Peter York first identified the phenomenon of the Sloane Ranger. The label lived on long after Lady Diana Spencer and her tribe had stopped wearing strings of pearls and upturned collars.

 When the editors of counterculture magazine Oz invited schoolchildren to edit its May 1970 issue, they were heading for trouble. Richard Neville, Felix Dennis, centre, and Jim Anderson were prosecuted over its sexually themed cartoons and articles. Photograph: Popperfoto/Getty Images

Style punditry quickly became a major engine of the industry as the 1980s came to an end. Alongside the Face and Esquire, GQ, a magazine that celebrated its 25th birthday last week, set out to target a sophisticated, young audience. The US parent magazine was bought by CondÃ© Nast in 1983, and the company launched several international editions. In 1998, the UK edition began its enduring, high-profile quest to find the men "whose own peers expect to make a major impact in the year ahead" and its Men of the Year awards enjoyed their 16th outing two months ago. Past winners make an eclectic line-up: Michael Douglas, Russell Brand, Sir Bradley Wiggins, athlete Steve Backley, writer Kazuo Ishiguro and Patrick McLoughlin, now secretary of state for transport. In 1998, the event was marked with a drinks reception; this year there was a black-tie dinner at the Royal Opera House.

The capacity for smaller publications to take the most important risks has been proved repeatedly over the last century, not least last year when the Oldie was the first to repeat in print the rumour that Jimmy Savile had committed child abuse, alluded to in Miles Goslett's feature about the BBC's aborted Newsnight item.

Similarly, in 1992, the subversive Private Eye printed the allegation that the heart surgery unit at Bristol Royal Infirmary was known as the Killing Fields because of high death rates: "Despite a long crisis of morale among intensive-care staff, the surgeons persistently refused to publish their mortality rates in a manner comparable to other units." Editor Ian Hislop said: "It followed that pattern of Eye stories where you have to keep on telling it and eventually it comes good."

In a Britain where many of the young men who once hunched over a copy of NME have now graduated to comparing dishwasher reviews in the consumer magazine Which?, Nicholas Coleridge of publisher CondÃ© Nast has spoken of a potential new golden age for magazine journalism. Sales of printed copies may have dropped, but he believes the public are still devoted readers of their favourite titles.

"Circulation figures don't show that people enjoy reading them on the new devices," he told BBC Radio 4's The Media Show last week, adding that his leading title, Vogue, has had its "most profitable year" yet, and that a veteran such as House and Garden sells in the same numbers it did 50 years ago.

Talking to the Professional Publishers Association in the runup to its centenary, Coleridge recalled his early, independent days working for editor Tina Brown on Tatler. The publication was, he said, "so broke that, in order to pay the restaurant critic Julian Barnes, one of my jobs was to ring up publishers and pretend we were doing a feature on illustrated books. They would duly bike over all these fabulous volumes, which I'd then take in a taxi to a secondhand book dealer called D Levin of Grape Street, unload them, flog 'em, come back with the cash, and hand (most of it) to Tina. She'd then hand it to JB and he would go off somewhere like the Connaught for lunch and pontificate about it."

Nicholas Coleridge (second left) recalls review copies of books were sold to pay restaurant critic Julian Barnes. In the middle, in the hat, is editor Tina Brown. Photograph: Graham Morris/Getty Images

Hudson, who compiled the PPA's 100 moments in consultation with contacts across the industry, believes the magazine business still relies on bringing content to people in a way they can enjoy, whether online or in print.

While new electronic platforms allow for much more involvement with readers, she warned editors of the future to keep a clear idea what they want to create.

"There is so much more customer feedback now, but then there is also a danger that you only give customers what they want and not what they don't realise they want yet," she said.

Barry McIlheney, the PPA's chief executive and a former editor of music title Smash Hits and movie magazine Empire, is also heartened to see that reports of the death of print magazines have been exaggerated.

"I kept being told that print was over at conferences, but it doesn't seem to be true," he said. "Things look very different now to even five years ago. Print magazines go very deep into the affections of their readers and many are also working really well on tablets: a high percentage of the top 50 rated apps are magazines."

While McIlheney regrets that Smash Hits is no longer on the newsstands and selling a million copies an issue, he celebrates the survival of Empire, which he launched. "It is still going stronger than ever."

    Obviously an industry body is going to go into boosterism overdrive in a centenary year.

    But the consumer magazine industry today is fighting a rearguard action. Eyeballs and advertising are draining from the printed page to the screen, with no like-for-like revenue replacement even for those publishers who have created successful digital platforms for their magazines.

    It gets worse.

    The magazine industry is not recruiting young readers in anything like the numbers it needs to stand still, never mind grow. Significantly, The Oldie far outsells NME, and Private Eye outsells the formerly million-selling FHM by over 2 to 1.

    Nor is the magazine industry recruiting young talent. Smart young people see the industry as fading in relevance, impact, importance and prospects, so for media wannabes it is now very much a career of last rather than first resort.

    If the industry is recruiting fresh blood at all in today's era of closures, redundancies, pay cuts and rate cuts, it has to make to with the mediocre, adding to an anxious, confused and defensive workforce increasingly composed of old lags hanging on by their fingernails.

    From such a talent pool, there is little hope of fresh and effective creativity, while publishers â€” themselves no longer the brightest and the best â€” fool nobody with guff about brands while trying to put a brave face on managing decline.

    I salute exceptions to this broad picture of decline like CondÃ© Nast, who have always invested in talent and fresh ideas. And Gill Hudson and Barry McIlheney are highly talented people of great integrity who temperamentally seek out a bright side.
