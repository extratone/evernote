The UpTake: Medium, which turn a year-old this month, has writers, editors and everybody else who deals in sentences asking a lot of questions: What is it exactly? Is it a publication? Is it a platform? Is it both? How will it make money?

I hate the word content. It is the worst word on the Internet.

What is content? To me, content belongs in a jar. On the Internet it’s everything from BuzzFeed listicles to corporate blogs to The New York Times's “Snowfall”.

This is a problem for me, a content creator who writes a lot about content. But when I see headlines like “If software is eating the world, Medium is eating its content” I feel a looming sense of dread.

I’m not alone. Medium, a startup built by Twitter co-founders Evan Williams and Biz Stone that turns one year old this month, has the content world flummoxed.

Medium itself isn’t complicated. It’s very intentionally the anti-Twitter, a clean, easy-to-use platform (the Internet’s second most terrible word) for longform writing. Writing is organized into thematic collections, where users (it's invite-only right now) submit their work. Relative to the rest of the Web, the quality is fairly high. Medium’s editorial team curates the site’s best writing and the company has even commissioned some stories.

Even better, Medium’s stripped-down minimalist design means that even if the substance of your content is terrible, it still looks amazing. Random trolling curated from the dregs of Reddit would look pretty legit pasted into Medium.

People expect great things from Medium, which makes sense given its truly disruptive pedigree. From my vantage point, it’s already playing an outsize role in the discourse in some tech circles, to the point where at least one writer took to Medium to criticize its inward-looking Silicon Valley bent. And it’s true, many of the collections are dedicated to the kind of high-minded ruminations on the zen of entrepreneurship that readers of this blog are probably familiar with.

Medium’s unorthodox, new agey management style — Holacracy — has also attracted attention. Hint: it does not include managers.

But here’s what creators want to know: how is Medium going to make money? Well, that’s the trick isn’t it? Nobody really knows what Medium’s game is other than the fact that its founders are really, really smart.

Writer Andrea Phillips raised the question of author rights in a post on Medium in July (questioning Medium is something of a mini-genre on Medium)

Phillips wrote:

Medium is just an upscale version of the same old business model used by shady content farms the world around. And it contributes to an environment where the most important element in creating written content — the writer — is undervalued and uncompensated.

Her main concern, Medium’s terms of service, which give the company "non-exclusive worldwide, royalty-free, sublicensable, transferable license to utilize all copyright rights now in existence or that may arise in the future with respect to your User Content." Some attempt to monetize this content is inevitable, Phillips feels.

Evan Williams recently told Bloomberg Businessweek that the business plan is in flux. His answer to Medium’s profit-model sounds like a lot of longform experimenters, we’ll see what works.

Brad Stone of Businessweek writes:

Medium isn’t developing a conventional ads-for-page-views model—Williams says he can’t imagine a banner ad on the site. To change the dynamic of online writing, however, the company has to figure out a way to make money and pay its writers. (Matter, a similarly minded science and technology journalism site that Medium acquired earlier this year, charges readers 99¢ per month and pays contributors.) Evan Hansen, a former Wired editor who joined Medium as a senior editor last spring, says the startup is exploring approaches that include reader subscriptions and ad sponsors for specific article collections, a model similar to Mutual of Omaha’s Wild Kingdom on Animal Planet.

There’s plenty of uncertainty about whether the company will offer publishing tools for writers or employ journalists (or both). One of Medium’s more ambitious ideas is to create a marketplace for editorial services, such as fact-checking, copy editing, and photo editing, through which authors or Medium can pay pros to do those jobs on individual stories.

At any rate, there’s going to be a lot of experimentation. Most recently, Medium partnered with Epic Magazine, a kind of longform journalism studio geared at pitching the rights to film-worthy stories to Hollywood.

Finally, there’s the question of whether Medium is a platform or a publication, and if is a publication, what responsibility does it have when it comes to plagiarism, accuracy and all those other J-School abstractions.

Or could it be the vanguard of something new and different? Alexis C. Madrigal at The Atlantic suggests.

So what is Medium? Medium is a place to read articles on the Internet. Medium is a blogging platform, like Wordpress or Blogger. Medium is the new project from the guys who brought you Twitter. Medium is chaotically, arrhythmically produced by a combination of top-notch editors, paid writers, PR flacks, startup bros, and hacks.

Is it the publication for our particular moment?

There’s a lot to chew over here, but I wonder if this hybrid publication can completely own the conversation in the same way as Twitter. We’re already seeing publications try to adopt Medium’s more innovative features before it becomes a serious threat.

Quartz is allowing commenters to annotate paragraphs and The New York Times is experimenting with tweetable highlights from stories, both features are similar to features on Medium. And when it comes to commenting tools, Nick Denton at Gawker Media may have everyone beat with Kinja.

What Medium becomes is as good story as there is, but whether it features writing or content is an open question.

One that’s going to get a lot of play on Medium.
