Like I said in my last email, I'm pursuing dual-majors in Business and Journalism, emphasizing specifically in marketing and strategic communication. Although I've always really enjoyed writing, when I arrived at the University in Fall 2014, I had recently been accepted in to a leadership program within the Truslake College of Business and started off only pursuing a marketing major, but pretty quickly realized that I was in need of a more creative outlet, specifically one that allowed me to write, and I began sneaking in an extra journalism class or two each semester until I was officially a dual-major student. 

I became involved with the Department of Student Activities and their concert booking committee within my first month on campus, and I was promoted to Committee Chair in January 2016 where I served for one semester. Afterwards, I accepted a promotion and served as Marketing Director for two more semesters and a summer, overseeing promotion for all six of DSA's committees. 
Post-graduation, I've always envisioned myself working within the music industry, whether it be in booking and promoting concerts or festival, or working directly for artists, helping them execute their creative visions.

My personal music tastes are all over the board. I think back to being freshly 16, driving almost everyday to my local library where I would check out five or six albums, import them into my iTunes library and consume and explore them like it was my job. In hindsight, many of my most formative musical opinions and tastes come from that summer in high school, where I used the library's collection to build my own, diving deep into iconic artists and classic albums, and eventually branching off to more obscure genres and eras. Today, my curiosity remains just as wild. I can discuss the genius of My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy with the same passion and attention-to-detail that I'd use to discuss Pet Sounds. 

I jokingly refer to myself as a pop-apologist. Top 40 radio can always be disappointing, but I think far to often critics and fans alike confuse pure pop music and see it synonymous with the radio-ready, factory-designed hit songs on Top 40 stations and discredit the entire genre. The idea that a song can be lyrically and sonically worthwhile and still work on a large commercial scale is super exciting to me. Robyn's BodyTalk is one of my favorite albums ever, and I think it deserves the same attention and devotion as any classic album, but because it's bubblegum, poppy songs that could work on radio, it might often be overlooked. I think, if you feel that it would fit and make sense in Extratone, that pwould be the direction I would be interested in pursuing.
