MACSTORIES COLLECTIONS

Videogame Grab Bag
John: The iPhone is one of my favorite places to play ports of classic games or modern, retro-style games, especially when they incorporate game controller support. Although I have a wide variety of handheld game devices, it's still hard to beat the iPhone's gorgeous screen paired with a Backbone One controller. So today, I thought I'd share a handful of the games I've been playing recently.

Dadish

Not long ago, I covered Dadish 3, the latest in the Dadish series of pixelated platformers. The games are free to play with ads but include an in-app purchase option to remove them. Although I've been aware of the Dadish series, I hadn't played one until I tried Dadish 3, which I loved, so I downloaded all three and started at the beginning with the first game.

I'm generally not a fan of ad-based games, but Dadish handles its ads well, giving you a sense of what the game is like before interrupting the fun with an ad, which was enough for me to get a sense of the game and decide it was worth paying $2.99 to remove the ads and keep playing. The story is simple: you play as Dadish, a radish dad in charge of his rambunctious radish children who take off across the game's many levels and need to be found. It's an absurd plot that's really well implemented with excellent animation, funny dialogue, and a cheerful, upbeat soundtrack.

The controls are simple. Playing in landscape orientation, you tap on the left and right sides of a divider on the left side of the screen to move and on the right side of the screen to jump and double-jump. The onscreen controls aren't bad, but I prefer using the Backbone One, where the D-pad controls movement, and the A button is used for jumping.

The early levels are simple, providing a good introduction to the types of puzzles you'll need to solve and the obstacles you'll encounter as you search for your radish children. There are typical platformer obstacles like spikes and saw blades, along with various buttons and switches that trigger elements needed to advance through a level. While not novel, the combination of playful art and a great soundtrack elevates Dadish above other similar games, so I plan to work my way through the entire trilogy over the course of the summer.
Dadish's fun dialogue helps make the game special.

Phoenix Wright: Ace Attorney Trilogy

The Ace Attorney Trilogy is a classic trio of games that first debuted about 10 years ago. The games, Phoenix Wright: Ace Attorney, Phoenix Wright Ace Attorney: Justice For All, and Phoenix Wright Ace Attorney: Trials and Tribulations, are the first three games in the popular crime-solving series and were remastered a few years ago for the Nintendo Switch, Xbox One, and PS4, but on iOS, players were limited to Ace Attorney Trilogy HD until earlier this summer when Capcom updated the graphics for iOS and Android. Unfortunately, save data from the earlier version of the trilogy, which was removed from the App Store, can't be transferred to the new version.

I'd never played an Ace Attorney game, so I figured the iPhone's big, bright screen would be a good way to dip my toes into the franchise. So far, I'm loving it. The games don't have controller support, but because the series is essentially a point-and-click mystery adventure, that hasn't been a big deal. Instead, I've enjoyed the incredibly campy mysteries and oddball characters that the series is so well-known for. The games, which were made for the Nintendo 3DS, benefit from being mobile-friendly stories that are enjoyable in small chunks of time too. The series isn't cheap at $25, but that's still less than you'd pay for it on a console, which makes it the most economical way to get into the series.

Some of the many colorful characters in the Ace Attorney series.

Streets of Rage 4

Streets of Rage 4 is a follow-up to the street fighting series published in the early 90s on Sega consoles. The latest installment of the series was released in 2020 for consoles and PC and was an immediate hit. I picked up the game a couple of months ago when it was released on iOS and Android and have been playing it on and off ever since. The side-scrolling fighter game features multiple characters you can play, punching and kicking your way through each stage.

I don't have much of a history with classic arcade fighting games, but I've enjoyed Streets of Rage 4. The fighting mechanics aren't overly difficult to learn, and it works well for short play sessions, which is something I always appreciate on the iPhone. Also, the game supports controllers and syncs saves using iCloud, which is far too rare among iOS games, and undoubtedly thanks to the fact that the iOS version was developed by Playdigious, the same studio that created Dead Cells, one of my all-time favorite games on any platform.

Game Dev Story and Game Dev Story+

Game Dev Story is a simulator game where you run a game development studio, which as to be the most meta storyline possible for a videogame. I've been playing Game Dev Story, which I bought on the App Store ages ago, but the '+' version is available for Apple Arcade subscribers. Like many simulation games, your job is to manage limited resources to try to keep your employees happy and engaged as you grow your company, making games. I love the pixel art and the endless variable that can be tweaked, but fair warning, this game will suck you in and won't let go. It's a great distraction that has the potential of becoming an unhealthy obsession. Fortunately, unlike many games in this genre, it isn't jam-packed with microtransactions, so the only thing it will cost you is your time and your initial $4.99 investment.

Exploring topics beyond our day-to-day coverage.

Back to Reeder with a Split RSS Solution
John: My approach to RSS and what I need from it have changed significantly over the years. RSS used to be where I found all of my tech-related reading material. Today, there are fewer interesting small and medium-sized sites to follow than there once were, and I get my reading material from more sources, including Twitter, the Club MacStories+ Discord, and more. That led me to rethink my RSS setup, which I wrote about in the January Monthly Log. Since then, the experiments have continued, and the solution has evolved, so I thought it would be good to provide an update.

What hasn't changed is that I still treat RSS as an inbox that I process with Reeder. There are a lot of good alternatives to Reeder, but in the end, I'm always drawn back by Reeder's design. The app doesn't have the power user features of lire or Fiery Feeds, Unread rivals its design but doesn't quite suit me, and ReadKit adds some gesture and design niceties that other apps haven't matched, but Reeder's combination of features and design is hard to beat and just fits well with my reading habits.

The biggest change over the past several years is in the feeds I follow, which break down into two categories. The first is what I call Apple & General Tech, a long list of sites that cover Apple along with others that cover tech more broadly like The Verge, TechCrunch, Wired, and Ars Technica. This bucket of feeds is separated into the Apple-focused sites that I read most closely and the general tech sites where I tend to speed-skim headlines, looking for unique stories and topics that I'm interested in or want to link in Weekly.

My Apple & General Tech feeds used to be synced between devices using Inoreader. It's a great power-user RSS service, but I found myself using fewer and fewer of the service's saved searches and filters that I once relied on heavily. That's the difference in my RSS browsing that led me to use Reeder's iCloud sync instead of a dedicated sync service for the first time in years, and to my surprise, the change has stuck.

I've found that Reeder sometimes fails to properly sync a handful of stories I marked as read on one of my devices, but in practice, that hasn't mattered much because all I'm doing with these tech sites is skimming headlines and a few extra headlines don't slow me down in a meaningful way. Another factor may be that seeing many of the same articles on Twitter and elsewhere has made me more tolerant of seeing duplicate headlines.
The second set of feeds that I follow is related to media. This is where I read about music, TV shows, movies, videogames, and pop culture. I don't follow these sites nearly as closely, which meant the unread count on the feeds often got enormous compared to my tech feeds, so, as I described in January, I decided to separate my media feeds from my tech feeds by using Reeder for tech sites, and Fiery Feeds for everything else.

The trouble with that approach was that once those feeds were out of sight, they were out of mind too. I read them less than ever. As a result, I've moved my media feeds back into Reeder but with a twist. Instead of syncing those feeds with the same service I use for tech feeds, I moved my media feeds to Feedly.
What I like about the Feedly solution is that I follow far fewer media feeds than I do tech feeds, which allows me to use Feedly's free tier. The other benefit of using a separate account is that I have to swipe over to Reeder's first column, which is usually hidden. That way, I don't have to stare down those large unread counts all day. At the same time, though, my media feeds are much more accessible because they're all still in Reeder, which I have open most of the time.

So far, the new setup is working better than the first iteration of the solution that I tried in January. I skim the tech headlines multiple times a day but only check my media feeds every few days. I follow several high-volume media feeds, but I don't follow them to read everything. Instead, just like the tech feeds, I scan for interesting stories. However, unlike the tech feeds, if I get behind on the media feeds, I just mark them all as read and move on, figuring that if something is interesting enough, it will bubble up somewhere else too. I'm sure this setup will continue to change, but reunifying my feeds in one app in a way that lets me keep them visually separated has been an excellent way to simplify and streamline my headline skimming.

APP DEBUTS
Noteworthy new app releases and updates, handpicked by the MacStories team.

Craft

John: Daily notes are a popular feature of many modern note-taking apps, including Craft, because they're so flexible. You can log what you do daily, keep a journal, track tasks, and much more. With the latest update to Craft, it's easier to manage incomplete tasks. Before, if you didn't complete a task in one daily note, there wasn't a good way to kick it to a later date.

However, Craft users can now move incomplete tasks to various predefined dates or a custom date using the app's task progress circle. Tasks can also be marked as complete or canceled in bulk. The developers have added similar functionality to move a text block from one daily note to another. Alternatively, blocks can be dragged and dropped onto any date in the app's calendar to move them between daily notes. The update also introduces new formatting features like block-level statistics and copying and pasting of text formatting.

Cubox

John: Not long ago, Federico mentioned on AppStories that he was revisiting Cubox, an iPhone, iPad, and Mac app for organizing webpages and clippings, images, audio, video, and more. The app includes Safari extensions, supports Shortcuts, and has powerful search for navigating large collections of materials, making it an interesting option for web-based research.

This week, Cubox 7.0 was released with an extensive update to its tagging system. The app now supports nested tagging and has an all-new tag editor. Also noted in the latest release notes is that an international version of the app is coming soon, which I'm looking forward to seeing since much of the app's help system and API documentation is in Chinese.

OmniFocus 3

John: Apple Voice Control is an accessibility feature of the iPhone, iPad, and Mac that the latest release of OmniFocus takes advantage of to deliver voice-driven interaction with the app. The new Voice Control features are built into Omni Automation, The Omni Group's proprietary JavaScript-based automation system, allowing users to access all of OmniFocus' core functionality by voice. You can learn more about what is possible on the Omni Automation website.

GameTrack

John: GameTrack is my favorite way to track the videogames I'm playing. There are many different ways to track this sort of thing, but GameTrack has won my heart with its videogame-specific features and design, which I love.

Version 4.0 of GameTrack is an excellent update that makes managing your videogame collection and playing easier than ever. The update consolidates your lists into a new Library tab on the iPhone where you can quickly access all of your games, lists you've created, as well as gaming accounts for PlayStation, Xbox, and Steam. Those accounts can be used to automatically add games to GameTrack now. I signed into my PlayStation accounts, and dozens of games were immediately available in the All Games list, making it easy to then move them to any of the lists I'd created. Lists have been updated too. A game can be added to multiple lists, and the iconography and color of lists can be customized.

Raycast

John: One of the things I appreciate most about the Mac launcher app Raycast is that it's constantly evolving, with two updates a month going back months now. Version 1.38 was released this week, and it's a big one, introducing several new design changes that I like a lot. There's a new icon that resembles a keyboard key, an appropriate choice for the keyboard-driven app.

The changes go deeper, though. The app's UI has been revamped with a bigger search bar that anchors its interface when invoked and visually separates it from the results that appear as you type. Raycast's command for accessing contextual actions (⌘K) is more prominent, too, as are actions associated with making it easier for new users to discover what the app can do and the keyboards associated with actions.

Finally, Raycast has added a space-saving compact mode for those who don't want to see all the options available when the app is activated, and there is new iconography throughout, which, to my eye, is more readable and balanced with the other window elements.
