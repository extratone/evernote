1. Stanzas. Half-stanzas. Pairs. Odd and even lines.

Each stanza of dróttkvætt consists of eight lines (vísuorð), and is divided into two half-stanzas (helmingar) of four lines each. There is always a syntactic break between the two half-stanzas. A distinction must be made between odd lines (1,3,5,7) and even lines (2,4,6,8), because of their structural differences (see below). An odd line forms a pair with a succeeding even line.

2. Syllables. Stress. Positions.

Dróttkvætt is basically a syllabic meter. Normally, each line consists of six syllables, three of which are stressed. The last two syllables always form a distinctive unit, composed of a strong (long, stressed) syllable and a weak (short, unstressed) one, but the rhythm of the first four syllables can vary considerably. The simplest type of line consists of a regular alternation of strong (S) and weak (w) syllables, Sw Sw Sw:

1:3 DRJÚGR var LOPTR at LJÚGa

with stressed syllables in the first, third, and fifth positions; unstressed syllables in the second, fourth, and sixth positions (Type A).

While the last two syllables always conform to this rhythmical pattern (Sw), which might be considered as trochaic, the first part of the line can exhibit a great variety of rhythmical possibilities. There is no agreement among scholars regarding these possibilites. See Appendix.

3. Alliteration. In-rhyme.

The dróttkvætt meter employs both alliteration and assonance. The alliteration binds the lines into pairs, while the assonance (in-rhyme) occurs within the same line.

3a. Alliteration.

Each odd line contains two alliterating stuðlar ("props"), which furthermore alliterate with the höfuðstafur ("main staff") in the next even line, forming a pair of lines:

1:1 Flugstalla réð Felli
1:2 Fjörnets goða at hvetja

1:3 drjúgr var Loptr at Ljúga
1:4 Lögseims faðir heiman

The latter of the two stuðlar almost invariably falls in position five (the penultimate syllable), while the höfuðstafur always occupies the first syllable of the line. Any vowel (or diphthong) can alliterate with any other vowel. Initial J- is treated as a vowel. Identical consonants alliterate, and the groups SP-, ST-, and SK- are treated as single phonetic units.

2:7 Endr til Ymsa kindar
2:8 Iðja setrs frá Þriðja

8:7 Jarðar skafls af Afli
8:8 Áss hretviðri blásin

9:5 háðu STáli STríðan
9:6 STraum hrekkmímis ekkjur

3b. In-rhyme.

Each line of dróttkvætt contains two internal rhymes (hendingar: frumhending and viðurhending). The second of these (viðurhending) almost invariably falls in position five (the penultimate syllable), while the position of the first (frumhending) varies. Typically, there is a difference between the in-rhymes of odd and even lines. The odd lines usually contain half-rhymes (skothendingar), while the even lines contain full-rhymes (aðalhendingar):

9:5 hÁÐu STáli STrÍÐan
9:6 STraum hrEKKmímis EKKjur

2:7 ENDr til Ymsa kINDar
2:8 IÐJa setrs frá ÞrIÐJa

However, full-rhymes may be found in odd lines:

1:3 drJÚGr var Loptr at LJÚGa

8:7 Jarðar skAFLs af AFLi

It must be noted that reflexes of the u-umlaut (a, ja > ö, jö) were considered to rhyme fully with their non-umlauted counterparts:

5:2 vANN fetrunnar NÖNNu

4. Word order. Imbedded and intertwined sentences. Tmesis.

The dróttkvætt meter is notorious for its convoluted word order and fragmented sentences. One sentence may be embedded within another, and even intertwined with another sentence in a manner that defies logic. Combined with other complexities of the form (e.g. kennings), this often results in stanzas which can be interpreted in multiple ways.

This convoluted word order (including an embedded sentence) is clearly demonstrated by the following:

2:1 geðstrangr of lét göngu
2:2 gammleið Þóarr skömmum
2:3 [fýstusk þeir at þrýsta
2:4 Þorns niðjum] sik biðja

which, in prose, would read:

geðstrangr Þóarr of lét gammleið skömmum biðja sik göngu; þeir fýstusk at þrýsta Þorns niðjum.

The above example is fairly normal. In the below example we find a main clause and a subordinate clause intertwined with each other in a devious manner:

7:5 þverrir lét [nema þyrri]
7:6 [Þorns] barna sér mörnar
7:7 [snerriblóð] til [svíra]
7:8 salþaks megin vaxa

which, in prose, would read:

þverrir mörnar barna lét sér megin vaxa til salþaks, nema snerriblóð svíra Þorns þyrri.

This principle of fragmentation could also apply to composite words, which could be split into their components (tmesis), as the following example demonstrates. The split word is gallópnis:

3:6 gall- mantælir halla
3:7 -ópnis ilja gaupnum

5. Kennings.

The poets of dróttkvætt employed an intricate system of metaphoric circumlocution, usually referred to by its original name, kenning ("paraphrase"). A simple kenning is defined as a phrase, consisting of a base noun (stofn "stem"), qualified by another noun in the genitive (kenniorð "qualifier"). The qualifier typically transforms the meaning of the stem:

svanr blóðs "swan of blood" = raven

Here, the stem svanr "swan" could be replaced by any bird's name, but the qualifier blóðs "of blood" turns the swan into a raven, the typical carrion-bird (bird of the battle-field) of the kenning-system.

The qualifying noun (blóðs) was easily replaced by new kennings, thus:

svanr sveita sverðs "swan of sweat of sword" = raven
svanr sveita sára "swan of sweat of wounds" = raven

Here, blood is paraphrased as "sweat of sword", and "sweat of wounds". Both are perfectly permissible: blood may be compared with sweat, either as dripping from a sword, or as dripping from a wound.

In an actual dróttkvætt stanza, we find:

svanr sveita þorns sára "swan of sweat of thorn of wounds" = raven

This is equivalent to svanr sveita sverðs (above). The sword has been replaced by a new kenning þorn sára "thorn of wounds".

The above example, svanr sveita þorns sára "swan of sweat of thorn of wounds", has been chosen to illustrate the problems of interpreting kennings in dróttkvætt. The four words: svanr, sveita, þorns, sára, could occur anywhere within a half-stanza, not obviously related to each other. An attempt to read svanr sveita sára "swan of sweat of wounds" would leave the word þorns unaccounted for (and tempt the reader to relate it to another, unrelated part of the half-stanza). The reader might also be tempted to interpret sveiti sára þorns in a totally different manner. Þorn is often used to mean "giant", and is equivalent to þurs. In the system of kennings, any giant's name could be replaced with any other giant's name. Thus, sveiti sára Þorns "sweat of wounds (blood) of the giant" could easily be a kenning for the ocean, which mythologically originated from the blood of Ymir, the primeval giant. Such an interpretation would leave the word svanr unaccounted for (and tempt the reader to relate it to another, unrelated part of the half-stanza).

The intricacies of the system of kennings cannot possible be dealt with in a short introduction. Let us finish this superficial treatment with an exaggerated (but real) example:

gimslöngvir drífu gífrs hlémána blakks nausta = warrior

i.e. "fire-brandisher of snowstorm of troll of protection-moon of horse of boat-shed". Working backwards, blakkr nausta "horse of boat-shed" = ship. The ship's hlémáni "protection-moon" = shield. The shield's gífr "troll" = sword. The sword's drífa "snowstorm" = battle. The battle's gim "fire" = sword. The sword's slöngvir "brandisher" = warrior. (Gimslöngvir is equivalent to slöngvir gims).

6. Appendix: The metrics of dróttkvætt.

At the end of the 19th century, and in the beginning of the 20th, a number of scholars participated in a lively (and sometimes vitriolic) debate regarding the metrics of dróttkvætt. Eduard Sievers, Andreas Heusler, Finnur Jónsson, E. A. Kock, Konstantin Reichardt, and Hans Kuhn, were the major players. After the tempest, the scholars' interest seems to have dwindled drastically, until Kuhn published his Das Dróttkvætt in 1983. His analysis has hardly met any serious challenges. Kristján Árnason offered a new interpretation in his The Rhythms of Dróttkvætt and Other Old Icelandic Metres in 1991. The most recent, and most important analysis, however, is Kari Ellen Gade's The Structure of Old Norse Dróttkvætt Poetry (1995).

It is interesting to note that Sievers' analysis is still very much alive. Kuhn's analysis (1983) is basically an extention of it; Árnason's interpretation relies on it; and Gade, even though she is extremely critical of Sievers, Kuhn, and Árnason, chooses to present her analysis within the framework of the Sievers/Kuhn model.

It is my opinion that Sievers' model of the five basic types is still quite valid, even though it is by no means a final, acceptable analysis of the dróttkvætt meter. Kuhn, Árnason, and Gade, have pointed out the flaws; but the fact remains that Sievers, despite the criticisms of his model, still remains the best "starting-point" for anyone who wants to delve into the murky regions of the metrics of dróttkvætt.

Below, I will attempt to show how Sievers' five types (A, B, C, D, E) can be applied to any line of Þórsdrápa. It must be noted that Sievers hardly paid any attention to alliteration and in-rhymes, which must have added an additional pattern of stresses. Kuhn (1983) revised Sievers' model extensively, and Gade (1995) reformulated it in a more satisfactory manner. Unfortunately, Gade omitted Þórsdrápa from her analysis, deeming it "too corrupt for a sensible reading without too many conjectures" (pp. xii, 267).
